Learning random variables as a high-schooler is quite easy, 
even in my first course of Probability theory, a 
random variable $X$ was just a map from $\Omega \to \RR$. 
Everything was nice and easy until the time $X$ was taking 
countably many values, but then there was a need of 
$\sigma-$algebras. There was no looking back; we had reached 
a point where everything required a proof. So today, I'll 
save everyone from the seemingly unexpected terror of
random variables.

Just to be more precise going forward, we are going
to prove measurability of events(subsets of $\Omega$) and
functions from $\Omega \to \ol\RR$ where $\ol\RR$ is the 
set of extended reals.

# The Updated Definitions

Anyone learning axiomatic probability-theory, first learns
about the probability space only being about the 
sample space $\Omega$ and the probability function
$\P\colon \mathcal{P}(\Omega) \to [0,1]$. But the moment you
tackle uncountable probability spaces, then one must resort
to the use of the sigma-field $\mathcal{F}$, to filter out
the _"good sets"_ and work with them.

<Definition title="Probability Space">

A probability space refers to the tuple
$(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \P)$ where $\Omega$ is some 
set(sample space), $\mathcal{F}$ is some $\sigma-$algebra
over $\Omega$ and $\P$ is a function from
$\mathcal{F} \to \RR$ satisfying

1. $\P(A) \ge 0$ for all $A \in \mathcal{F}$;

2. $\P(\Omega) = 1$;

3. If $\{A_k\}_{k = 1}^\infty$ is a sequence of pairwise disjoint sets where $A_k \in \mathcal F$ for $k \ge 1$, then
$$
\sum_{k = 1}^\infty \P(A_k) = \P\left( \bigcup_{k = 1}^\infty A_k\right).
$$

</Definition>

With the above new definition of the probability space, what
could possibly go wrong with random variables? Well, what
if you want to compute $\P(X \in B)$ where
$B \subseteq \RR$? Recall that
$$
[X \in B] \coloneqq \{\omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) \in B \}.
$$
If we really want to compute the probability of 
this event, then we need to make sure that this event
$[X \in B]$(we will also denote this by $X^{-1}(B)$) lies
in $\mathcal{F}$. If you saw the construction by Vitalli 
from the web-page which I shared above, then you basically 
saw that you cannot really assign a uniform 
probability over $[0,1]$. Thus, rather than computing the 
probability of events $[X \in B]$ where $B \subseteq \RR$, 
we restrict $B$ to lie in $\mathcal{B}(\RR)$. We are now
ready to define the notion of an ordinary random variable
over the measurable space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$.

<Definition title="Random Variables">

$X$ is said to be a random variable defined on a measurable
space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ if $X$ is a function
from $\Omega \to \RR$ such that
$$
X^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}\,\, \text{for all }B \in \mathcal{B}(\RR).
$$

where $X^{-1}(B) = \{\omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) \in B \}$
and $\mathcal{B}(\RR)$ denotes the Borel $\sigma-$algebra 
over $\RR$.

</Definition>

We have only defined an _ordinary random variable_ where
$X$ can't take values like $\pm \infty$. Often times, in 
cases of markov chains and other stochastic processes, 
we sometimes denote the return time of a particle by a 
random variable $X$. If the particle never returns then we 
set $X(\omega) = \infty$ in that case, so we will now 
define what is an extended random variable. From here,
we shall now denote
$\overline{\RR} = \mathbb{R} \cup \{\pm \infty \}$.

<Definition title="Extended Random Variables">

$X$ is said to be an extended random variable defined on a
measurable space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ if $X$ is a
function from $\Omega \to \overline\RR$ such that
$$
X^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}\,\, \text{for all }B \in \mathcal{B}(\overline\RR).
$$
</Definition>

Its a perfectly valid question to ask how does the 
borel sigma field over $\overline\RR$ looks like... For 
some beginners, they might be unfamiliar with the notion 
of borel-sigma algebra. Let's talk about it briefly 
before moving onto the actual results to be proven for 
random variables.

## Borel Sigma Algebras

Due to the construction by Vitalli, we know that not all
subsets of $\RR$ can be assigned a probability with the 
function $\P$. Thus we resort to what we call the 
Borel $\sigma-$algebra over $\RR$. Consider the usual metric 
topology[^1] on $\RR$ where let $\tau$ denote the set of all
open sets[^2] of $\RR$. Then we define 
$$
\mathcal{B}(\RR) \coloneqq \sigma(\tau)
$$
where $\sigma(\tau)$ means the [smallest $\sigma-$algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%A3-algebra#%CF%83-algebras_generated_by_families_of_sets)
containing all the elements of $\tau$. Although, dealing 
with all open intervals is quite cumbersome when you have 
to prove results. Luckily, $\RR$ is quite forgiving and we 
have a wonderful result, which is good enough that 
we state it as a theorem.

<Theorem title="Open sets of reals">

Any open set $U \subseteq \RR$ can be written as a disjoint
countable union of open intervals.

</Theorem>

<div class="proof">

See [on stackexchange](https://math.stackexchange.com/a/396299). $\quad\square$
</div>

With this, we have the luxury to define the borel-sigma 
algebra way more freely. We will now state a really 
really important lemma. If you really want to prove this
by yourself, then you can find an important lemma 
[here in the appendix](#generator-algebra).

<a id="borel-generator"></a>
<Lemma title="Alternate Definition">

On $\RR$, the $\sigma-$field generated by

1. Open Intervals
2. Open Sets
3. Closed Intervals
4. Closed Sets
5. Intervals of form $(a,b]$ where $a,b \in \RR$
6. Intervals of form $(-\infty, a]$ where $a \in \RR$.
7. Intervals of form $(-\infty, a)$ where $a \in \RR$.
8. Intervals of form $[a,\infty)$ where $a \in \RR$.
9. Intervals of form $(a,\infty)$ where $a \in \RR$.

all generate $\mathcal{B}(\RR)$. By generate, we mean
for example, look at 4th point, the smallest sigma algebra
containing all closed intervals is $\mathcal{B}(\RR)$.

</Lemma>

_Outline of Proof:_ This one requires some practice with 
sigma-algebras, which is not what we are doing in this
blog. Note that 2nd point is true is by definition. By 
the theorem stated above, 1st point becomes true as well.
For the other points, you need to take some countable union
of open sets to generate an arbitrary closed interval.
For example, if you want to make the closed interval
$[a,b]$ from open sets, then consider 
$$
\bigcap_{n = 1}^\infty \left(a-\frac1 n, b + \frac1 n \right)= [a,b].
$$
So, you can construct any closed interval from an open
intervals. The other parts can be done similarly. $\square$

If we want to talk about $\mathcal B (\ol\RR)$, then we 
need to characterize the open sets of $\ol \RR$, which 
is more or less a question of topology. If someone is 
interested in characterizing the open sets of $\ol\RR$, 
then you can refer the appendix one possible approach.
For this blog post, we will work a simpler definition of
$\mathcal B (\ol\RR)$, which is the following.

<Lemma title="Borel Sets over Extended Reals">

Define
$$
\mathcal{C} \coloneqq \{[-\infty, x]: x \in \ol\RR\}
$$
where $[-\infty, x] \coloneqq (-\infty, x] \cup \{-\infty\}$, 
then 
$$
\mathcal{B}(\ol\RR) = \sigma(\mathcal C).
$$

</Lemma>


Even with all this, I will give an alternate definition
to the borel sigma algebra over $\ol\RR$ in the appendix 
over extended reals. Anyways, we are now ready to head to
the more important results for random variables.

# Important Results

We will start by proving the following theorem.

<Theorem>

Let $\{X_n\}_{n \ge 1}$ be a sequence of ordinary real-valued
random variables. Show that

1. For $a \in \RR$, $aX_1$ is a random variable;

2. $|X_1|$ is also a random variable;

3. By giving a suitable example, prove that if $|X_1|$ is 
a random variable, then $X_1$ is not necessarily a random
variable;

4. $\max(X_1, X_2)$, $\min(X_1, X_2)$, $X_1 + X_2$ are all
random variables;

5. $\displaystyle \sup X_n$ and $\inf X_n$ are extended
real value random variables;

6. $\displaystyle\limsup_{n \to \infty} X_n$ and
$\displaystyle\liminf_{n \to \infty} X_n$ are extended real
value random variables;

7. For $A \in \mathcal{F}$, $X_1\bm{1}_A + X_2\bm{1}_{A^c}$
is a random variable.

</Theorem>

Before starting the proof, we recall the collection 
$B = \{(-\infty, x]: x \in \RR \}$ generates the entire 
$\mathcal{B}(\RR)$ thus $\mathcal{B}(\RR) = \sigma(B)$.
Thus to show that any map $f\colon \Omega \to \RR$ is a
random variable, it's enough to show that
$f^{-1}\left((-\infty, x] \right)\in \mathcal{F}$. If you 
want a rigorous proof as to why only checking it for 
generators suffices, then you will find it in the
appendix.

_Proof to part 1:_ We shall first show that $aX_1$ is a
random variable. If $a = 0$, then 
$$
(aX_1)^{-1}(-\infty, x] = \{ \omega : 0 = aX_1(\omega) \le x\}
$$

If $x < 0$, then then $(aX_1)^{-1}$ is precisely
$\emptyset \in \mathcal F$. If $x > 0$, then $(aX_1)^{-1}$ 
is precisely $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$. This shows 
that $aX_1^{-1}(-\infty, x] \in \mathcal F$ for all
$x \in \RR$. This case is done.

For the next case, assume $a > 0$. Once again, consider
$(aX_1)^{-1}(-\infty, x]$ which is precisely
$$
(aX_1)^{-1}(-\infty, x] = \{\omega \in \Omega: aX_1(\omega) \le x\} = \{\omega \in \Omega: X_1(\omega) \le x/a\} = X_1^{-1}(-\infty, x/a]
$$
Since $X_1$ is a random variable,
$X_1^{-1}(-\infty, x] \in \mathcal F$ completing the proof 
for this case as well.

The last case is slightly tricky. This time dividing by $a$ 
would change the sign of equality. So, we will work 
with another class of generators, that is
$\{[x, \infty): x \in \RR \}$. Consider 
$$
(aX)^{-1}[x,\infty) = \{ \omega: aX(\omega) \ge x \} = \{\omega: X(\omega) \le x/a \} \in \mathcal F
$$
since $X$ is a random variable and
$B = \{(-\infty, x]: x \in \RR \}$ is also a generator.
The proof is now complete. $\square$

<hr style="margin: 1rem 0;"/>

_Proof to part 2:_ We will stick with class of generators
$B = \{(-\infty, x]: x \in \RR \}$. Consider
$$
|X_1|^{-1}(-\infty, x] = \{\omega \in \Omega : |X_1(\omega)| \le x \}
$$
If $x < 0$, then $|X_1|^{-1} = \emptyset \in \mathcal F$.
If $x \ge 0$, then 
$$
|X_1|^{-1}(-\infty, x] = \{\omega \in \Omega : -x \le X_1(\omega) \le x \} = \{\omega\in\Omega : X_1(\omega) \in [-x,x] \}.
$$
Even in this case, if you consider the class of generators
only consisting of closed sets, then one would realize 
$|X_1|^{-1} \in \mathcal F$ in this case as well. The proof
is complete now. $\square$

<hr style="margin: 1rem 0"/>

_Counter Example to part 3:_ Consider $\Omega = \{ 0, 1\}$
and $\mathcal F = \left\{\{\emptyset \}, \Omega \right\}$
and consider a map $Y\colon \Omega \to \RR$ such that
$Y(0) = 1$ and $Y(1) = -1$. In this case, $|Y|$ is indeed 
a random variable, but $Y$ is not. To see this, note that
$\{ 1\} \in \mathcal B(\RR)$ and $Y^{-1}(\{ 1\}) = \{0\}
\not\in \mathcal F.$

<hr style="margin: 1rem 0"/>

_Proof to part 4:_ We will first prove $\min(X_1, X_2)$ 
is a random variable. Consider
$$
(\min(X_1, X_2))^{-1}(-\infty, x] = \{\omega \in \Omega: \min(X_1(\omega), X_2(\omega)) \le x \}.
$$

But for any two reals $a,b$, $\min(a,b) \le x \iff a \le x$
or $b \le x$. This gives
$$
(\min(X_1, X_2))^{-1}(-\infty, x] = \{\omega \in \Omega: X_1(\omega) \le x \} \cup \{ \omega \in \Omega: X_2(\omega \le x)\}
$$

Since $X_1, X_2$ are random variables, both the events in 
the union above lie in $\mathcal F$, thus their
intersection would lie there as well. We omit the prove
for $\max(X_1, X_2)$, but the idea to prove that is very
very similar.

Let $Y = X_1 + X_2$. We shall now prove that $Y$ is a 
random variable. This one truly tricky and uses a typical 
analysis trick which exploits that $\RR$ is separable[^3].
For this proof, we will use a different class of generators,
namely $\{ (x, \infty) : x \in \RR \}$. Now, consider
$$
Y^{-1}(x, \infty) = \{ \omega : X_1(\omega) + X_2(\omega) > x \}.
$$

Since $\QQ$ is countable, let $\{q_i\}_{i = 1}^\infty$ be 
an enumeration of $\QQ$. We have the following claim.

<Claim> $\displaystyle Y^{-1}(x,\infty) = \bigcup_{i = 1}^\infty \{\omega: X_1(\omega) > q_i\} \cap \{\omega: X_2(\omega) >x - q_i \}.$ </Claim>

The proof to the claim is extremely simple. If
$\omega \in Y^{-1}(x, \infty)$, then we know
$$
X_1(\omega) > X_2(\omega) - x
$$
Since these two quantities are different, by density of 
$\QQ$ over $\RR$, we know there must exist some index $j$
such that 
$$
X_1(\omega) > q_j > X_2(\omega) - x
$$
which immediately gives $\omega \in \bigcup_{i = 1}^\infty \{\omega: X_1(\omega) > q_i\} \cap \{\omega: X_2(\omega) >x - q_i \}.$ The other direction is rather trivial, so we shall 
skip it.

We can now finish the proof, since $X_1$ and $X_2$ are 
random variables and $\{ (x, \infty) : x \in \RR \}$ is 
a class of generators, we get that both events
$\{ \omega: X_1(\omega) > q_i \}$ and $\{\omega: X_2(\omega) > x - q_i \}$ both lie in $\mathcal F$ for all $i \in \NN$.
The intersection would lie in $\mathcal F$ and so would a 
countable intersection! The proof is now complete. $\square$

<hr style="margin: 1rem 0"/>

_Proof to part 5:_ Define $Y\colon \Omega \to \ol\RR$
such that $Y(\omega) = \sup_n X_n(\omega)$. We shall
use the generator of $\ol\RR$, which is the collection
$\{ [-\infty, x]: x \in \RR\}$. Thus, consider 
$$
Y^{-1}[-\infty, x] = \left\{ \omega : \sup_n X_n(\omega) \le x \right\}
$$

Now, recall that $\sup_n b_n \le x \iff b_n \le x$ for all
$n \in \NN$ where $b_n$ is a sequence of reals. With this, 
we get the following
$$
Y^{-1}[-\infty, x] = \bigcap_{n = 1}^\infty\{\omega: X_n(\omega) \le x \}
$$

Since $X_n$ are random variables(which means they extended 
random variables as well), we know that $\{\omega: X_n(\omega) \le x\} \in \mathcal F$ for $n \in \NN$. So does their
countable union! This shows that $Y$ is an extended random
variable as desired.

We'll give a small sketch for the infimum part, this time
consider the collection $\{ [x, \infty]: x \in \RR\}$ which
generates $\mathcal B (\RR)$. This time use the fact that
$\inf b_n \ge x \iff b_n \ge x$ for all $x \in \RR$ where
$b_n$ is a real sequence. This concludes the proof. $\square$

<hr style="margin: 1rem 0;"/>

_Proof to part 6:_ We shall only prove it for $\limsup X_n$.
Once again define $Y\colon \Omega \to \ol\RR$ given by
$\displaystyle Y(\omega) = \limsup_{n \to \infty}
X_n(\omega)$. Define another sequence of functions
$Z_n\colon \Omega \to \ol\RR$ given $Z_n(\omega) =
\sup_{k \ge n} X_k(\omega)$ for all $n \in \NN$. By
definition of $\limsup$, we know
$$
Y(\omega) = \inf_{n}\sup_{k \ge n} X_k(\omega) = \inf_n Z_n(\omega)
$$

We shall prove that $Z_n$ is an extended random variable, 
then by the result proven above, we must have that
$Y = \inf Z_n$ is also a random variable. One needs to be
careful; in the previous proof we assumed that $X_i$'s are
real valued random variables, then $\inf X_n$ is an
extended random variable. In our case, we have a sequence
of extended random variables $Z_n$ and then we are 
concluding $\inf Z_n$ is also a random variable. The proof
of this completely the same as above and can be worked out
quite easily, so we'll skip it here.

<Claim> $Z_n$ is an extended random variable for all $n \in \NN$.</Claim>

<div class="proof">

Using the generator $\{ [-\infty, x] : x \in \RR \}$ of 
$\mathcal B (\ol\RR)$, consider
$$
Z_n^{-1}[-\infty, x] = \left\{ \omega: \sup_{k \ge n} X_k(\omega) \le x \right\}
$$

A standard analysis fact says that $\sup_{n \ge k}
X_k(\omega) \le x \iff X_j(\omega) \le x$ for all $j \ge n$.
This gives, 
$$
Z_n^{-1}[-\infty, x] = \bigcap_{j = n}^\infty \{\omega : X_j(\omega) \le x \}.
$$

Since $\{ \omega: X_j(\omega) \le x \} \in \mathcal F$ for
all $j \ge n$ since $X_j$ is a random variable(thus an 
extended one too) for all $j \in \NN$, we get that 
countable intersection must lie $\mathcal F$. Done. $\square$
</div>
This completes the proof to part 6.

<hr style="margin: 1rem 0"/>

_Proof to part 7:_ We'll use our favourite class 
of generators, namely $\{ (-\infty, x]: x \in \RR \}$.
Define $Y\colon \Omega \to \RR$ such that 
$Y(\omega) = X_1\bm{1}_A(\omega) + X_2\bm{1}_{A^c}(\omega)$
for all $\omega \in \Omega$. Once again, we want to show 
$Y$ is a random variable.
$$
Y^{-1}(-\infty, x] = \{\omega : X_1\bm{1}_A(\omega) + X_2\bm{1}_{A^c}(\omega) \le x\}
$$

The idea is simple, just check cases whether $\omega \in A$
or $\omega \not\in A$. This yields
$$
Y^{-1}(\infty , x] = \{\omega: X_1(\omega) \le x, \omega \in A \} \cup \{ \omega: X_2(\omega) \le x, \omega \in A^c\}
$$

The final trick here is to notice that 
$$
Y^{-1}(-\infty , x] = \left(\{\omega: X_1(\omega) \le x\}\cap A \right)\cup \left(\{ \omega: X_2(\omega) \le x\} \cap A^c\right)
$$

Clearly, $Y^{-1}$ is a union of two measurable sets. This
concludes the proof. $\square$

This concludes a series of very important results, which 
are pretty much used everytime while you are dealing with
random variables. Here are a couple of more important results
which are must to know. As a matter of fact, this 
is quite an important theorem which I previously 
used in my blog post regarding the
[Ky-Fan Metric.](/writings/posts/ky-fan-metric) The details
and the reference of this theorem is mentioned in the blog

<Theorem>

Let $X_n$ be sequence of real-valued random variables, then

1. $\{ \omega \in \Omega: \lim_{n \to \infty}X_n(\omega) \in \ol\RR  \} \in \mathcal{F};$

2. $\{ \omega \in \Omega: \lim_{n \to \infty}X_n(\omega) \in \RR  \} \in \mathcal{F};$

3. Define $A = \{ \omega \in \Omega: \lim_{n \to \infty}X_n(\omega) \in \ol\RR  \} \in \mathcal{F}$.
Consider a map $X\colon \Omega \to \ol\RR$

$$
X(\omega) = 
\begin{cases}
\lim_{n \to \infty} X_n(\omega) \qquad &\text{for }\omega \in A \\
0 \qquad &\text{for }\omega \in A^c.
\end{cases}
$$
then $X$ indeed an extended random variable;

4. Define $B = \{ \omega \in \Omega: \lim_{n \to \infty}X_n(\omega) \in \RR  \} \in \mathcal{F}$.
Consider a map $X\colon \Omega \to \RR$

$$
X(\omega) = 
\begin{cases}
\lim_{n \to \infty} X_n(\omega) \qquad &\text{for }\omega \in B \\
0 \qquad &\text{for }\omega \in B^c.
\end{cases}
$$
then $X$ indeed an random variable.

</Theorem>

We will now begin with the proofs.

_Proof to 2nd part:_ This one is easy, just recall the 
cauchy criteria for convergence over $\RR$. We say a 
real sequence $a_n$ is cauchy iff for every $k \in \NN$
there exists some $N \in \NN$ such that
$|a_m - a_n| < \frac 1 k$ for $m,n > N$. We know that
$\lim a_n$ exists finitely iff $a_n$ is cauchy. With this
criteria, we may write 
$$
\{ \omega \in \Omega: \lim_{n \to \infty}X_n(\omega) \in \RR  \} = \bigcap_{k=1}^\infty \bigcup_{N = 1}^\infty \bigcap_{n = N}^\infty \bigcap_{m = N}^\infty \left\{\omega : |X_m(\omega) - X_n(\omega)| < \frac 1 k \right\}
$$

Well, since $|X_m - X_n|$ is a random variable for all
$m,n \in \NN$, thus the set $\left\{\omega : |X_m(\omega) - X_n(\omega)| < \frac 1 k \right\}$ lies in $\mathcal{F}$.
Under countable union and intersection, the final 
set remains in $\mathcal{F}$ completing the proof. $\square$

<hr style="margin: 1rem 0;"/>

_Proof to part 1:_ We will take the help of part 2 this time.
We have already resolved the case when the limit lies in
$\RR$, so we need to handle the case when limit is
$\pm \infty$. We may write
$$
\{ \omega \in \Omega: \lim_{n \to \infty}X_n(\omega) \in \ol\RR  \} = \{ \omega \in \Omega: \lim_{n \to \infty}X_n(\omega) \in \RR  \} \cup \{\omega\in \Omega: \lim_{n \to \infty} X_n(\omega) = \pm \infty \}
$$

The first set of the union lies in $\mathcal F$. For the
second part, we may partition the set into cases 
where limit is $\infty$ or $-\infty$. We will only give a 
proof for limit being infinity here. Its easy, just 
observe
$$
\{ \omega \in \Omega: \lim_{n \to \infty}X_n(\omega) = \infty \} = \bigcap_{M = 1}^\infty \bigcup_{N = 1}^\infty \bigcap_{n > N} \{\omega \in \Omega: X_n(\omega) > M \}.
$$
Now, $\{\omega \in \Omega: X_n(\omega) > M \} \in \mathcal F$
since $X_n$ is a random variable for all $n \in \NN$. 
After taking countable union and intersection, we still 
get a set a in $\mathcal{F}$ completing the proof. $\square$

<hr style="margin: 1rem 0;"/>

_Sketch to proof of part 3:_ Consider $X^{-1}$ which is
$$
X^{-1}(x,\infty] = \{\omega: X(\omega) > x \} = \{\omega: X(\omega)> x, \omega \in A \} \cup \{\omega: X(\omega) > x, \omega \in A^c \}
$$

The above can be re-written as
$$
X^{-1} = \{ \omega \in A: \lim_{n \to \infty} X_n(\omega) > x \} \cup (\{\omega: 0 > x \} \cap A^c)
$$

It is easy to see that $\{\omega: 0 \le x \} \cap A^c \in 
\mathcal{F}$. It suffices to show that
$\{\omega \in A: \lim_{n \to \infty} X_n(\omega) > x \} \in \mathcal F$. 
Now, $\lim_{n \to \infty} X_n(\omega) > x \iff
X_n(\omega) > x$ for all $n > N$ where $N \in \NN$. This
means, 
$$
\{\omega \in A: \lim_{n \to \infty} X_n(\omega) > x \} = \bigcup_{N = 1}^\infty\{\omega: X_n(\omega) > x \,\,\text{for $n > N$}\}
$$
This concludes the proof since $\{\omega: X_n(\omega) > x \,\,\text{for }n> N\}$ for all $n \in \NN$ since $X_n$ is a
random variable. $\square$

To end the blog, I'll present the final a very significant
result, which answers questions like, if $X$ is a random 
variable then should $\sin(X)$ should be a random variable? 
What about simpler thing like $X^2 + 4X$?

<Theorem title="Continuity preserves Measurability"> 

Let $X$ be a random variable on the measurable space
$(\Omega, \mathcal F)$. Let $f\colon \RR \to \RR$ be a 
continuous function in its domain, then $f(X)$ is a 
random variable as well.

</Theorem>

<div class="proof">

Denote $A$ as set of all finite open intervals of $\RR$ 
which generates $\mathcal B(\RR)$ and let $(a,b) \in A$.
Consider 
$$
f(X)^{-1}(a,b) = \{\omega \in \Omega: f(X(\omega)) \in (a,b)\} = \{\omega \in \Omega: X(\omega) \in f^{-1}(a,b)\}
$$

Recall a simple fact from analysis/topology, a pull-back 
map of a continous function takes an open set back to an 
open set, which means $f^{-1}(a,b)$ is an open set. Since
open sets generate $\mathcal{B}(\RR)$, the set above 
set lies in $\mathcal{F}$ concluding the proof. $\quad\blacksquare$

</div>

Indeed, a natural generalization follows. Instead of saying 
$f$ continuous, if you imposed the condition that 
$f$ was a measurable function from $\RR \to \RR$, i.e., 
for any Borel set $B$, $f^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{B}(\RR)$, 
even then the conclusion holds true.

## Two Essential Lemmas for Sigma Fields

The following lemma is quite extensively used(honestly 
quite easy to prove as well) to prove if two collections
$\mathcal{C}_1$ and $\mathcal C_2$ generate the same 
$\sigma-$algebra or not. This result is exclusively used 
in proving [this lemma](#borel-generator) where we have 
discussed about several generators of $\mathcal B(\RR)$.

<a id="generator-algebra"></a>
<Lemma>

For a measurable space $(\Omega, \mathcal F)$, let
$\mathcal C_1$ and $\mathcal C_2$ be two collections of 
subsets of $\Omega$. Then
$$
\sigma(\mathcal C_1) = \sigma(\mathcal C_2) \iff \mathcal C_2 \subseteq \sigma(\mathcal C_1) \quad \text{and} \quad \mathcal{C_1} \subseteq \sigma(\mathcal C_2).
$$

</Lemma>

The following theorem allows us to prove that a certain 
map from $\Omega \to \RR$ is a random variable. The special
thing is, we can choose whatever generator we want, and 
it would still suffice. A version of the theorem 
for general measure spaces can be found [here on page 24.](https://www.math.uci.edu/~rvershyn/teaching/2007-08/235A/course-notes-probability.pdf#35#5)

<Theorem>

For a measurable space $(\Omega, \mathcal F)$, let
$\mathcal C_1$ be a collection of subsets of
$\RR$ such that it generates $\mathcal{B}(\RR)$, i.e.
$\mathcal{B}(\RR) = \sigma(\mathcal C_1)$. Then a map
$X\colon \Omega \to \RR$ is a _random variable_ if and only 
if
$$
X^{-1}(C) = \{ \omega \in \Omega: X(\omega) \in C \} \in \mathcal{F}\quad \text{for all } C \in \mathcal C_1.
$$

</Theorem>

<div class="proof">

The _only if direction_ is quite trivial. We will prove 
the other direction, assume that 
$$
X^{-1}(C) \in \mathcal F \quad \text{for all}\quad  C \in \mathcal C_1.
$$

Define
$$
\mathcal{T} \coloneqq \{C \in \sigma(\mathcal C_1) : X^{-1}(C) \in \mathcal F \}.
$$

Its obvious that $\mathcal T \subseteq \sigma(\mathcal C_1)$.
By definition of $\mathcal T$, its also immediate that
$\mathcal C_1 \subseteq \mathcal T$. Here's the main 
claim.

<Claim>$\mathcal T$ is a $\sigma-$algebra over $\Omega$.</Claim>

<div class="proof">

We'll only sketch the proof. What you basically need, are 
the properties of a _pull-back_ map. By our assumption, 
$X^{-1}(\{\emptyset\}) \in \mathcal F$, thus
$\{\emptyset\} \in \mathcal T$. Let $A \in \mathcal T$, then
$$
A \in \sigma(\mathcal C_1) \implies X^{-1}(A) \in
\mathcal F.
$$
Since $\mathcal F$ is a $\sigma-$field, we 
know that $(X^{-1}(A))^c \in \mathcal F$. Its a well-known
property of a pull-back map that
$(X^{-1}(A))^c = X^{-1}(A^c)$. This gives
$X^{-1}(A^c) \in \mathcal F$ which proves
$A^c \in \mathcal T$.

Verifying countable union is ommitted, one just needs to 
prove that
$$
X^{-1} \left( \bigcup_{n = 1}^\infty A_n\right) = \bigcup_{n = 1}^\infty X^{-1}(A_n)
$$
for $A_n \in \mathcal T$ for all $n \ge 1$. $\square$

Now, recall that $\mathcal C_1$ lies in $\mathcal{T}$
which is a sigma algebra and
$\mathcal{T} \subseteq \sigma(\mathcal C_1)$. Since
$\sigma(\mathcal C_1)$ is minimal sigma field containing
$\mathcal C_1$, we must have $\mathcal T =
\sigma(\mathcal{C_1})$, completing the proof.
$\quad \blacksquare$

</div>

</div>


## Conventions on Extended Reals

This section is rather short. But in case of $\ol\RR$, we 
define 
$$
0\times \pm\infty = \pm\infty \times 0 = 0
$$
for our probability purposes. The reason for this, is to 
define expected value. If an extended random variable 
$X$ takes values $\pm\infty$ with probability $0$, then we 
essentially want to treat it as an orindary random variable
over $\RR$. For this to truly hold, you need to define 
the multiplication of infinity with $0$ to be $0$.

## Borel Sigma Algebra over Extended Reals

I'll brief the idea as to how someone can topologically 
describe open sets of $\ol\RR$.

We will construct a homeomorphism in a topological
sense(this approach is Exercise 33, in Chapter 1 of [A Probability Path](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-1-4612-1818-0) by Sidney Resnick) from $[-1,1] \to \ol\RR$
given by the map
$$
x \mapsto \frac{x}{1-|x|}
$$

Thus in principle, what one can do it take all open sets 
in $\RR-$subspace topology of $[-1,1]$ and map it to a new
open set in $\ol\RR$. This would generate all your open 
sets in $\ol\RR$. An interested reader can go and look for 
the details online([here's a recommendation](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLpRE0Zu_k-Bz6I691nDbE0qLsCFKwx5ku) although, this one goes into measure theory), but we shall
skip it since it requires a good knowledge of metric spaces
and topologies induced by it.
